Why this matters

Early teams should not have to make critical decisions blindly.

Most early teams do not fail because people are not working hard. They fail because founders need to make important decisions all the time, often without experienced support nearby: hiring too early, building the wrong GTM motion, or trying to scale before repeatable systems exist.

The cost of getting these decisions wrong is rarely just money. It is time, energy, and often the confidence of the team.

The challenge is rarely a lack of effort. It is making important decisions while information is incomplete and time is limited. My role is to reduce that uncertainty and leave behind systems that continue working after I step away.

The pattern

Across startups, open source communities, operations and partnerships, the common thread has been the same: entering situations with high uncertainty and leaving behind something that continues working after I step away.

I work best in the early stages, when there is no playbook yet. I typically join for a defined period, help build and stabilise the first version of the machine, then transition ownership back to the team.

This is not consulting. I take ownership of outcomes for a defined window, not just responsibility for recommendations.
